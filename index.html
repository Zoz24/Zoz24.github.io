Man, You Can Figure Out Anything If You Try

So, it’s been about two years since I got my computer science degree, and I’ve been working in the field ever since. Lately, I’ve been thinking about what I’ve actually learned—you know, beyond all the technical stuff. I could ramble on about operating systems, computer architecture, AI, or whatever, but that’s not what this is about. This is about the one thing that’s hit me hard: if you really want to, you can figure out almost anything.

Back in college, everything felt like black magic. I’d sit in class, staring at this stuff—object-oriented programming, meta programming, whatever—and think, “No way I’m ever getting this.” It was like I was on the outside of some secret club. But then I started working, coding, messing up, fixing bugs, and something clicked. Not all at once, but over time. I’d be grinding on a problem, and then—boom—I’d get it. Like, “Oh, that’s what’s going on here.” All the layers just made sense, and I’d look back and go, “That wasn’t even that hard.”

That’s when I hit this epiphany moment. It’s not about being a genius or knowing all the jargon—React, Kubernetes, serverless functions, mobile dev, all that cool stuff. It’s about realizing that if you try, you can break it down and figure it out. Use the thing, learn how it works, dig into the architecture, maybe even get the philosophy behind it. Once you do that a few times, you’ve got a system. Next time something unfamiliar pops up, you just run the same playbook. Most of the time, it works.

And it’s not just coding. I’ve taken this mindset everywhere. Like, I didn’t know how to change my car’s oil. But I was like, “Screw it, I can figure this out.” Watched a YouTube video, checked the manual, and bam, I did it. Same deal with registering a company or filing taxes—stuff that sounds scary until you dig in. You don’t need to pay someone else; you just need to try.

Now, I’m not saying this works for everything. Quantum physics? String theory? Yeah, good luck—I’m not figuring that out anytime soon. And it’s different when you’re trying to create something brand new, like the next jet engine or a gaming console. That’s a whole other level. But understanding what’s already out there? The HTTP protocol, a jet engine’s basics, Android development—most of that is doable if you put in the work.

Looking back, I wish I’d known this in college. So much felt impossible because it looked crazy from the outside. But now, after two years of working and wrestling with code, my perspective’s flipped. Most stuff isn’t that hard—it just takes effort. You dig deeper, you apply it, and eventually, you’re like, “Yeah, I got this.” That’s the biggest thing I’ve learned, and it’s changed how I see everything.
